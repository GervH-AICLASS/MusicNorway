Ásatrú: Modern Norse Paganism

What is Ásatrú?

Ásatrú is a modern religion that revives, reconstructs, and reimagines the ancient polytheism of Northern Europe. It began in 1972 when Sveinbjörn Beinteinsson and eleven other Icelanders decided to bring back public worship of the Norse gods, goddesses, and land spirits.

The word Ásatrú is modern Icelandic for "Æsir Faith" and refers to belief in the major tribe of Norse deities. Practitioners often self-identify as Heathens.

Key Aspects of Ásatrú

  • No central authority or set dogma
  • Variety of beliefs and practices worldwide
  • Spans from humanism to reconstructionism
  • World-accepting religion focusing on right action in this life
  • Central belief: "we are our deeds"

Deities and Practices

Deities venerated include Odin, Thor, and Freya, among others. The central ritual is called Blót, which involves making offerings to gods, goddesses, land spirits, and departed people of importance.

Historical Context

The history of Northern European polytheism stretches from the Bronze Age through the Viking Age. Although large-scale practice ended with the coming of Christianity, some beliefs and rituals survived into the twentieth century as elements of folk religion.

Modern Recognition

Ásatrú is now Iceland's largest non-Christian religion. In the United States, the DOD or Department of Defense officially recognized Ásatrú and Heathenry in 2017, granting full religious rights to practitioners in all service branches.

Sources of Inspiration

Followers of Ásatrú draw inspiration from various historical texts, including Greek, Roman, and Arabic descriptions of Germanic peoples, myths preserved by medieval Icelandic and Danish writers, Old Norse and Anglo-Saxon poetry, Icelandic sagas, and folklore collections.
